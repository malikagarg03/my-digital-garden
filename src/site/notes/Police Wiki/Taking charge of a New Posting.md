# Taking Charge of a New Posting (Draft)

*These are my evolving notes on what I have learnt about taking charge of a new police unit. They are based entirely on my own experiences and mistakes. I expect these notes to change as I gain more experience.*

---

## The biggest mistake I made

When I first became an SDPO, I thought my job was to make decisions.

Looking back, that wasn't my biggest challenge.

My biggest challenge was that I simply didn't understand the subdivision.

I didn't know:

- What I should be paying attention to.
- How information should reach me.
- Which issues were genuinely important.
- Who could be relied upon.
- What the history behind each incident was.

I had information.

I didn't have context.

A Daily Situation Report could tell me that a murder had been registered.

It could not tell me whether this was an ordinary village dispute or something that could lead to retaliation, political intervention or communal tension.

That understanding lived in people's heads.

Over multiple postings, I gradually realised that the first responsibility of a new officer is not to make decisions.

It is to rapidly build an accurate mental model of the district.

---

# Leadership begins before you join

The day your posting order is issued, people start forming opinions about you.

The first impression is not created when you walk into your office.

It is created by everything you do before that.

If you ask for structured information before joining, officers immediately realise that they will be expected to prepare.

When I joined as SP, I asked each subdivision to prepare a presentation before I even arrived.

I also asked the District Police Office to compile various datasets and reports.

The purpose was not merely to collect information.

It was also to communicate that I intended to understand the district before I started making decisions.

---

# Your first job is to build situational awareness

I think many new officers feel pressure to demonstrate leadership immediately.

I now think that is the wrong objective.

The first few weeks should primarily be spent building situational awareness.

Every meeting, visit and briefing should help answer questions like:

- What are the major issues in this district?
- Who are the important personalities?
- Which cases continue to be sensitive?
- Which problems keep recurring?
- Which officers really understand their jurisdiction?
- Which issues are likely to become tomorrow's problems?

Only after building this picture should major decisions begin.

---

# Build a mental model, not a file

The objective is not collecting documents.

The objective is building an internal map.

When I asked subdivisions to prepare presentations, I wanted them to cover things like:

- Who's who.
- Major law and order issues.
- Sensational cases from the previous year.
- Important undetected cases.
- Important personalities.

These weren't random headings.

Together they begin creating a mental model of the district.

---

# Listen to multiple versions

Every person explains the district from their own perspective.

Special Branch sees one picture.

Local police see another.

Politicians have their own interests.

Journalists have another perspective.

Senior officers see things differently again.

None of them is completely wrong.

None of them should be accepted blindly.

One of the most important things I have learnt is to deliberately seek multiple perspectives before forming my own understanding.

Do not inherit someone else's picture of the district.

Construct your own.

---

# Find people who improve your thinking

In one of my postings I was fortunate to have an excellent DSP, Special Branch.

He rarely just gave me facts.

He explained the context.

He helped me understand why something mattered.

That experience also taught me something else.

Not every posting will have someone like that.

So I now consciously look for people who help me think better.

I don't want people to make decisions for me.

I want people with whom I can sound out important decisions and hear alternative perspectives.

The final decision should always remain mine.

---

# Build your own operating system

The first few weeks are not just about understanding the district.

They are also about designing how you will work.

One example is phone calls.

Immediately after joining, a large number of petitioners begin calling.

If you answer every call immediately, especially in an unfamiliar district or language, your attention becomes fragmented very quickly.

In one posting, I created a simple system.

If an unknown number called, I would send a screenshot to a trusted person/control room.

They would call the person back, identify:

- who they were,
- where they were calling from,
- what the issue was,

and send me a WhatsApp summary.

If it was genuinely urgent, they would immediately alert the concerned SHO as well.

I could then review these summaries periodically without constantly interrupting whatever I was doing.

The objective wasn't to avoid speaking to citizens.

It was to protect attention while ensuring that genuine emergencies received immediate action.

To ensure I don't miss out on important callers I asked for a VCF file containing contact details of:

- Police officers.
- Government officers.
- Politicians.
- Media persons.
- Other important stakeholders.

Importing these contacts before joining meant I could immediately distinguish important known callers from completely unknown numbers.

It sounds like a small thing.

It removes a surprising amount of friction.

---

# Call-ons are not just protocol

Call-ons (both us calling on others or others calling on us) are often treated as formalities.

I increasingly think they are one of the best opportunities to understand a district.

Use them to learn.

Ask questions.

Understand how different stakeholders see the district.

The Collector, judiciary, senior police officers, revenue officials, elected representatives and media all possess different pieces of the puzzle.

Don't use these meetings merely to introduce yourself.

Use them to build context and begin future collaboration.

---

# Be visible

A new officer should not disappear into the office.

People should know that someone has taken charge.

Visibility is not about publicity.

It is about leadership.

Meet your officers.

Visit police stations.

Complete your call-ons.

Interact with the media.

Attend important events.

The district should feel that leadership has changed.

---

# Keep a map in front of you

One habit I strongly recommend is keeping a district map visible during the first few weeks.

Whenever somebody mentions a village, a crime scene or a sensitive location, find it on the map.

Gradually connect geography with:

- police stations,
- subdivisions,
- roads,
- forests,
- rivers,
- recurring law and order issues,
- sensitive villages,
- important personalities.

Eventually the district stops being a collection of names.

It becomes a picture.

---

# Resist the temptation to act

Experience has taught me to avoid doing certain things immediately after joining.

I generally avoid:

- making transfers,
- introducing major new systems,
- conducting detailed reviews before understanding the basics,
- drawing conclusions after hearing only one version.

This doesn't mean delaying action indefinitely.

It means understanding before changing.

---

# One principle I keep returning to

Every posting has reinforced the same lesson for me.

The first month is not about exercising authority.

It is about building enough situational awareness that every later exercise of authority becomes better informed.

Everything else follows from that.
